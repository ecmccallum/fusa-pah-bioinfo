1.1 Overview

Raw paired end Illumina reads were evaluated using FastQC and summarized with MultiQC. Reads were then filtered and trimmed with fastp. A second FastQC and MultiQC analysis was performed on the trimmed reads to assess whether read quality was maintained or improved after filtering. Raw and trimmed MultiQC reports were generated separately to preserve a clear before and after comparison.

Interpretation

Per base sequence quality was excellent across the full 150 bp read length in both files. Mean Phred scores remained consistently high, with no clear decline toward the 3' end.

Per sequence quality scores were also strong, with most reads concentrated at high quality scores. This confirms the dataset is of high overall sequencing quality before filtering.

Per tile sequence quality failed in both files, indicating localised variation in quality across sequencing tiles, most likely reflecting an instrument or flow cell related artefact. The overall per base and per sequence quality metrics remain high, so this failure is not expected to substantially affect downstream analyses.

Per sequence GC content failed in both files. The GC distribution was bimodal, with a main peak at approximately 52% GC and a secondary peak around 67–69% GC. The main peak is compatible with the expected GC content of Fusarium solani species complex genomes. The secondary peak suggests the presence of an additional organism with higher GC content. Previous BLAST screening indicated possible contamination by Methylorubrum populi, a high GC methylotrophic bacterium commonly found in association with fungal cultures. This will be tested systematically at Stage 2 using Kraken2.

Adapter content passed for both files, suggesting most adapter sequence had already been removed by the sequencing provider.

Overrepresented sequences produced a warning for fusa_2 only. This warning resolved after trimming, suggesting it was caused by low abundance technical sequences rather than a biological issue.

1.4 fastp — Trimming and Filtering

fastp v1.3.3 was run on a dedicated compute node (pyrene-n030) via an interactive SLURM session:

srun --cpus-per-task=4 --mem=16G --time=02:00:00 --pty bash
fastp --in1 ~/fusarium_pah/data/raw/fusa_1.fq.gz \
  --in2 ~/fusarium_pah/data/raw/fusa_2.fq.gz \
  --out1 ~/fusarium_pah/data/trimmed/fusa_1_trimmed.fq.gz \
  --out2 ~/fusarium_pah/data/trimmed/fusa_2_trimmed.fq.gz \
  --detect_adapter_for_pe \
  --qualified_quality_phred 20 \
  --length_required 50 \
  --thread 4 \
  --html ~/fusarium_pah/results/fastp_report.html \
  --json ~/fusarium_pah/results/fastp_report.json

Only approximately 1.3% of reads were removed during filtering, confirming that the raw data was already of high quality. The duplication rate of approximately 21% is not unexpected for fungal genome sequencing and does not by itself indicate a severe library complexity problem.

1.8 Conclusions

The raw Illumina paired end reads were of high quality. Per base and per sequence quality metrics passed for both files, and only approximately 1.3% of reads were removed during fastp filtering. This indicates that the sequencing data required minimal quality-based remediation before downstream analysis.

Two persistent FastQC failures were observed. The per tile sequence quality failure most likely reflects localised sequencing tile effects rather than a dataset-wide quality issue. The per sequence GC content failure reflects a bimodal GC distribution, suggesting the presence of more than one sequence population in the dataset. Based on previous BLAST results, the high GC component may correspond to Methylorubrum populi contamination, although this requires systematic confirmation at Stage 2.

The files fusa_1_trimmed.fq.gz and fusa_2_trimmed.fq.gz are ready for contamination screening using Kraken2 before genome assembly.
